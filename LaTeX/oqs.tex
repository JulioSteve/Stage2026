\subsection[Open quantum systems]{Open Quantum Systems (OQS)}\label{OQS}
\setlength{\parskip}{2mm}\setlength{\parindent}{6mm}
In the previous part, we saw that one can fully describe a system, observables, states and their evolution, however, this description applies to the ideal case where the quantum system is considered isolated from any external interaction. Instead, in the OQS theory systems interact locally with an environment, which itself interacts with a wider environment, etc\ldots To avoid considering infinite chains of interaction, the theory truncates the environment to a local vicinity of the system. Thus, we approximate the total system $T$ as a closed quantum system, whereas the quantum system of interest $S$ couples with its environment $E$ and is called a quantum open system. This coupling with the environment thus gives rise to a dynamical regime distinct from that of closed quantum systems.

We will now follow a certain approach~\cite{Albarelli} in the derivation of the Lindblad master equation, using the \textit{input-output formalism}~\cite{GardinerCollett}, however, it is possible to use the \textit{collisional-model} instead~\cite{Ciccarello2017,Ciccarello2022}. Since a full derivation of the input-output formalism is mathematically challenging, we will only provide its key equations and their physical meaning; further details on the input-output theory are available in the Appendix~\ref{ann:IOF}. The approach of OQS we will follow here is not the standard approach~\cite{Breuer} to derive the results of OQS, but, it is already aiming towards continuous measurements results. In order to lighten formulae, physical units are rescaled so that $\hbar=1$ from now on. 

As a consequence of the explanations about system $S$ coupling with its environment $E$, the Hilbert space of study is divided into $\mathcal{H}_T=\mathcal{H}_S\otimes\mathcal{H}_E$. The system Hamiltonian $S$ depends on which physical system is studied, however, the formalism used states that the environment Hamiltonian is defined by
\begin{equation}
    H_E=\int_0^{+\infty} b^\dagger_\omega b_\omega \omega\mathrm{d}\omega,
\end{equation}
where the operators of the environment $b_\omega$ satisfy $\qty[b_\omega, b_\omega^\dagger]=\delta(\omega-\omega')$. Note that these operators are not proper annihilation/creation operators because $[b_\omega, b_\omega^\dagger]\neq\mathbb{I}$. This Hamiltonian is a continuum of bosonic modes $\omega\in\mathbb{R}^+$, constructed from the quantum harmonic oscillator\footnote{Hamiltonian of the quantum harmonic oscillator: $H(\omega)=\hbar\omega\qty(b^\dagger b+\frac{1}{2})$, where $\qty[b,b^\dagger]=\mathbb{I}$.} {\color{red} why?}. The coupling between $S$ and $E$ is treated as a perturbation (the interaction has a smaller impact on the dynamics than the free evolution), and so, is expressed by the interaction Hamiltonian defined by
\begin{equation}
    H_I = i\int_0^{+\infty} \sqrt{\frac{\kappa(\omega)}{2\pi}} \qty(c b_\omega^\dagger - c^\dagger b_\omega) \mathrm{d}\omega,
\end{equation}
where $c$ is the system operator, and, $\kappa(\omega)$ is the coupling strength between $S$ and $E$. This shape of interaction Hamiltonian comes from the Rotating Wave Approximation (RWA) {\color{red} source?}, valid for weak-coupling condition $\omega_0\gg\kappa$, where $\omega_0$ stands for the natural frequency of the system.
