\chapter{Continuously measured open quantum systems}\noindent
In the previous chapter, we went through the steps leading to the Lindblad master equation using the input-output formalism. In this derivation, the environment has been traced out to recover information on the system $S$ from the global coupled system, leaving an impact on the dynamics of $S$, crystallized in the expression of a dissipator $\mathfrak{D}$. In the following we will focus on the impact of continuously monitoring the environment on the dynamics of the system $S$. Indeed, states of system on which we do continuous measurement follow other differential equations than the Lindblad master equation. However, we will see in the following how any differential equation obtained from continuously monitoring the environment is strongly linked to the Lindblad master equation.

Specifically in Chapter I, we have developed the input-output formalism in which the system $S$ interacts sequentially in time with harmonic oscillators. Indeed, at each time the environment is modeled by an harmonic oscillator represented by the annihilation operator $B_t$. This operator combines all environmental frequency modes by construction, as defined in Eq.~(\ref{eq:inputmodes}). A fundamental property explained in Chapter I is that these operators are mutually uncorrelated; consequently, the environment is ``refreshed'' after each evolution, ensuring no memory effects or temporal dependence of environmental operators. We can therefore represent the evolution of our system as being coupled at time $t$ to $B_t$, evolving according to the Lindblad master equation and immediately after, at time $t+\dt$, uncoupling from $B_t$ to couple with $B_{t+\dt}$, evolving again, and so forth. Going one step further, we consider measurement of the environment after each coupled evolution step, as seen on Figure~\ref{fig:scheme}.

In this chapter, two kind of measurements will be presented: the photo-detection and the homodyne detection. For each case, the reader will be able to find graphical illustrations accompanying the equations, which have been generated in \verb|Julia| using the packages \verb|QuantumToolbox.jl| to simulate dynamics, and \verb|Plots.jl| to generate graphs.\\
While we expect different dynamics depending on the measurement process used, we will explain their underlying connection. The evolution of unmonitored open quantum systems is described by the Lindblad master equation, as seen in Section~\ref{OQS}. However, performing a measurement after each infinitesimal step makes the evolution dependent on each outcome, thereby altering the system's trajectory. If we consider the state of the system at time $t$, the state is conditional because of all the measurement outcomes from $t_0$ to $t$ and is therefore noted $\rho_c(t)$. This state is therefore called \textit{a quantum trajectory} because the set of measurement outcomes gives a specific dynamical trajectory. One can show that the Lindblad master equation can be rewritten to let conditional states appear relative to every possible conditional evolution~\cite{Carmichael}. This result shows that the Lindblad master equation is decomposable into quantum trajectories that evolve relative to it in the absence of monitoring; therefore, we refer to quantum trajectories as \textit{an unravelling of the source dynamics} because it is one trajectory among the many paths coming from the same equation. In this sense, when describing photo-detection or homodyne detection, we unravel the master equation relative to a specific measurement choice; nevertheless, \textbf{on average, they recover the same Lindblad master equation, i.e. the unconditional dynamics}. We will show this result in the case of photo-detection and homodyne detection below.\\
Note that in order to lighten notations, we stop specifying by $\tilde{\bullet}$ the interaction picture, however if a change of representation occurs, it will be specified. Furthermore, we work from now on in natural units where $\hbar=1$.

\section{Photo-detection}
\subsection[Photo-detection Unravelling]{Derivation of the stochastic master equation}\noindent
In the case where an open quantum system is continuously monitored by a photo-detector, the mathematical impact is a projection of the environment on the corresponding Fock basis $\qty{\ketbra{n}_t}_{n\in\mathbb{N}}$; indeed, photo-detection is a way to measure the number operator $B_t^\dagger B_t$ because the Fock basis is the eigenbasis of this operator. At first glance the measurement would only give information about the occupation number of the harmonic oscillator modeling the environment; however by performing a partial trace $\mathrm{Tr}_E$ over the total evolved state, we recover information on the system due to coupling. 

Specifically, the expression of the conditional state obtained at time $t+\dt$ will be detailed here. Let $\qty{\ketbra{j}_t}_j$ be a PVM (see Section~\ref{sec:qmt}), so that the measurement projects the environment with corresponding oscillator $B_t$ on the PVM element $\ketbra{j}_t$. We recall the results of Section~\ref{OQS}: the total evolved system is expressed as
\begin{equation}
    \bm{\varrho}_c(t+\dt)= U_I(t+\dt,t)\overbrace{\qty[\rho_c(t)\otimes\ketbra{0}_t]}^{\bm{\varrho}_c(t)}U_I^\dagger(t+\dt,t),
\end{equation}
where the subscript $c$ indicates the system state is conditional. If the condition is obvious, we choose not to write this subscript explicitly, instead we write for example $\rho_j$ if the outcome is $j$. The system in this calculation is indeed conditional because we consider the measurement already present in the evolution before time $t$. After the projection on $\ketbra{j}_t$ and after applying the partial trace $\mathrm{Tr}_E$, we find
\begin{equation}
    \rho_j(t+\dt)=\frac{\bra{j}_t\bm{\varrho}_c(t+\dt)\ket{j}_t}{p_j(t+\dt)},
\end{equation}
where $p_j(t+\dt)=\Tr{\bm{\varrho}_c(t+\dt)\qty(\mathbb{I}\otimes\ketbra{j}_t)}$. In fact, Eq.~(\ref{eq:expansion}) shows that only projections on $\ketbra{0}_t$ and $\ketbra{1}_t$ contribute to $\rho_j(t+\dt)$, which will be exploited hereafter for each measurement result separately.
\subsubsection{Measurement result 0}\noindent
From the expansion in Eq.~(\ref{eq:expansion}), we find
\begin{equation}
    \rho_0(t+\dt)=\frac{\bra{0}_t\bm{\varrho}_c(t+\dt)\ket{0}_t}{p_0(t+\dt)}= \frac{\rho_c(t)-\qty{\rho_c(t),c^\dagger c}\frac{k\dt}{2}}{p_0(t+\dt)}.
\end{equation}
The normalization is found using the same expansion, giving
\begin{equation}
    p_0(t+\dt)=1-\expval{c^\dagger c}_t k\dt,
\end{equation}
where we have defined the average labelled by $t$ as $\expval{\hat{O}}_t=\Tr{\rho_c(t)\hat{O}}$. In the infinitesimal limit, we can also expand the denominator in the evolved conditional state, giving
\begin{equation}
    \rho_0(t+\dt)\approx\bar{\rho}_0(t+\dt)\times\qty(1-\expval{c^\dagger c}_t k\dt),
\end{equation}
note that we used the same notation as in Section~\ref{sec:qmt}, where $\bar{\rho}$ indicates an unnormalized state (here in the interaction picture in particular). In particular here $\bar{\rho}_0(t+\dt)$ is the numerator of $\rho_0(t+\dt)$. Finally, by introducing the measurement super-operator $\mathfrak{H}[\hat{O}]\bullet=\hat{O}\bullet + \bullet \hat{O}^\dagger - \expval{\hat{O}+\hat{O}^\dagger}_t\bullet$, we can write that
\begin{equation}\label{eq:rho0}
    \rho_0(t+\dt) = \rho_c(t)-\frac{k\dt}{2}\mathfrak{H}\qty[c^\dagger c]\rho_c(t).
\end{equation}
\subsubsection{Measurement result 1}\noindent
For this outcome, the same calculation method than the one used before still applies, and the conditional (unnormalized) state is expressed as
\begin{equation}
    \bar{\rho}_1(t)=\bra{1}_t\bm{\varrho}(t+dt)\ket{1}_t=c\rho_c(t)c^\dagger k\dt.
\end{equation}
The normalization is then
\begin{equation}
    p_1(t+\dt)=\expval{c^\dagger c}_t k\dt.
\end{equation}
Combined, they give the normalized conditional state
\begin{equation}\label{eq:rho1}
    \rho_1(t+\dt)=\frac{c\rho_c(t)c^\dagger}{\expval{c^\dagger c}_t}.
\end{equation} 
\subsubsection{Mixing the results}\noindent
From a statistical perspective, the measurement outcome at time $t+\dt$ is a Bernoulli-like process, yielding either 0 with probability $p_0(t+\dt)$ or 1 with probability $p_1(t+\dt)$. The full set of results up to time $T$ is captured by the ``sum'' of results obtained between times $t_0$ and $T$: if for example we discretize time allowing to measure the system 30 times and that the sum of results is 20, it is obvious that it is composed of 10$\times$0 outcomes and 20$\times$1 outcomes. In this sense, we define the random variable $N(T,t_0)=\int_{t_0}^T \mathrm{d}N_t$, where in an infinitesimal slice of time $\dt$, the increment $\mathrm{d}N_t=N(t+\dt,t_0)-N(t,t_0)$ is either $0$ or $1$; this last observation leads to the fundamental relation: $\mathrm{d}N_t^2=\mathrm{d}N_t$.  In effect, $N(T,t_0)$ is a Poisson process and  $\mathrm{d}N_t$ is a Poisson increment\footnotemark[1]. With the probabilities found above, we can calculate the stochastic average of $\mathrm{d}N_t$ to get
\footnotetext[1]{A Poisson increment follows a Poisson distribution with rate $\Lambda_t\in\mathbb{R}$: $\forall n\in\mathbb{N},\quad\mathbb{P}\qty[\mathrm{d}N_t=n]=\frac{\Lambda_t^n}{n!}e^{-\Lambda_t}$. However, since we only have two possible outcomes (0 or 1), the rate is forced to be $\Lambda_t=0$ because the sum of probabilities gives 1. By setting $\Lambda_t=\lambda_t\dt\rightarrow 0$ in the infinitesimal limit, we can find that $\mathbb{P}\qty[\mathrm{d}N_t=0]\approx 1-\lambda_t\dt\rightarrow 1$ and $\mathbb{P}\qty[\mathrm{d}N_t=1]\approx\lambda_t\dt\rightarrow 0$. It is therefore trivial to show that $\mathbb{E}\qty[\mathrm{d}N_t]=\lambda_t\dt$, and also that $\mathbb{V}\qty[\mathrm{d}N_t]=\lambda_t\dt+\lambda^2_t\dt^2$.} 
\begin{equation}\label{eq:avPoisson}
    \mathbb{E}\qty[\mathrm{d}N_t]=0\times p_0(t+\dt)+1\times p_1(t+\dt) = \expval{c^\dagger c}_t k\dt.
\end{equation}
\textbf{Note that the expression of $\mathbb{E}\qty[\mathrm{d}N_t]$ is proportional to the expectation value $\expval{c^\dagger c}_t$: in a system where $c$ is the annihilation operator, it means that we can experimentally find the mean occupation number}.\\
Therefore, the Poisson increment allows to write the differential conditional state $\mathrm{d}\rho_c(t+\dt)$ with respect to each possible outcome: $\mathrm{d}N_t=0$ means that the differential is $\mathrm{d}\rho_0(t)=\rho_0(t+\dt)-\rho_c(t)$, instead $\mathrm{d}N_t=1$ means that the differential is $\mathrm{d}\rho_1(t)=\rho_1(t+\dt)-\rho_c(t)$. Consequently, 
\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{d}\rho_c(t)=\mathrm{d}N_t\qty[\rho_1(t+\dt)-\rho_c(t)]+\qty(1-\mathrm{d}N_t)\qty[\rho_0(t+\dt)-\rho_c(t)].
\end{equation}
We can inject the results of Eq.~(\ref{eq:rho0},\ref{eq:rho1}) in the last equation to obtain (in the Schrödinger picture):
\begin{equation}
    \boxed{\mathrm{d}\rho_c=-i\qty[H_0,\rho_c]\dt-\frac{k}{2}\mathfrak{H}\qty[c^\dagger c]\rho_c\dt + \qty(\frac{c\rho_c c^\dagger}{\expval{c^\dagger c}_t}-\rho_c)\mathrm{d}N_t},
\end{equation}
in which we have discarded terms of order $\mathrm{d}N_t\dt$ (this can be understood informally because $\mathbb{E}\qty[\mathrm{d}N_t]\propto\dt$ and $\mathbb{V}\qty[\mathrm{d}N_t]\propto\dt+o(\dt)$). This equation is called the photo-detection stochastic master equation. 

Finally, when derived for a pure state the expression simplifies to a Stochastic Schrödinger Equation (SSE):
\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{d}\ket{\psi_c}=\qty[-iH_0\dt+\frac{k}{2}\qty(\expval{c^\dagger c}_t-c^\dagger c)\dt + \qty(\frac{c}{\sqrt{\expval{c^\dagger c}_t}}-1)\mathrm{d}N_t]\ket{\psi_c},
\end{equation}
which conserves the purity of the state during the stochastic evolution. Note that we can derive the SME from the SSE using \ito~calculus rule~\cite{Albarelli}:
\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{d}\rho_c=\mathrm{d}\ket{\psi_c}\bra{\psi_c}
    +\ket{\psi_c}\mathrm{d}\bra{\psi_c}
    +\mathrm{d}\ket{\psi_c}\mathrm{d}\bra{\psi_c}.
\end{equation}
The importance of the SME and of the SSE is double because these equations also contain in average the \textbf{unconditional} dynamics, in particular for the SME: $\rho(t)=\mathbb{E}\qty[\rho_c(t)]$, where $\rho$ denotes the unconditional state.
\subsection{The unconditional evolution}\noindent
For now, we formally write $R(t)\equiv\mathbb{E}\qty[\rho_c(t)]$ and show that this is the unconditional state $\rho(t)$ for photo-detection. To do so, we use the expectation value of Eq.~(\ref{eq:avPoisson}), giving
\begin{equation*}
    R(t)=-i\qty[H_0,\rho(t)]\dt+k\mathfrak{D}[c]\rho(t)\dt,
\end{equation*}
which is exactly the Lindblad master equation, implying that $R(t)=\rho(t)$. Thus, 
\begin{equation}
    \mathbb{E}\qty[\rho_c(t)]=-i\qty[H_0,\rho(t)]\dt+k\mathfrak{D}[c]\rho(t)\dt=\rho(t).
\end{equation}
Therefore for photo-detection, we have shown that averaging over all the quantum trajectories reforms the unconditional Lindblad master equation. This property is also valid for homodyne detection~\cite{W96,Jacobs2006,Rouchon2022} and will be used without proper demonstration in the following section. 
\section{Homodyne detection}\noindent
In the previous section, we derived the SME in the presence of a photo-detector and showed its link to the Lindblad master equation. While photo-detection monitors the number of excitations, the homodyne detection targets field quadratures. This process involves mixing the signal with a strong local oscillator before measurement~\cite{W96}. By measuring the global output signal, we have access to the quadrature of the physical system of interest, see Figure~\ref{fig:homodyne}.\\
In the quantum world, this translates to projecting the environmental oscillator --- associated with $B_t$ --- on the eigenvectors of the quadrature operator $\hat{x}_\theta$. This operator is defined by $\hat{x}_\theta=(e^{-i\theta}B_t+e^{i\theta}B_t^\dagger)/\sqrt{2}$ where $\theta\in\mathbb{R}$, and verifies $\qty[\hat{x}_\theta,\hat{x}_{\theta+\frac{\pi}{2}}]=i$. Even if it is not possible to construct normalized eigenstates of this operator, we can build the states $\ket{x_\theta}$ satisfying $\hat{x}_\theta\ket{x_\theta}=x_\theta\ket{x_\theta}$ such that $\ketbra{x_\theta}{x_\lambda}=\delta(\theta-\lambda)$, and verifying the closure relation $\int_\mathbb{R}\ketbra{x_\theta}\mathrm{d}x_\theta=1$. We will use the following overlapping property~\cite{Barnett} between these states and the Fock states:
\begin{equation}
    \braket{x_\theta}{n}_t = \frac{e^{-\frac{x_\theta}{2}}H_n(x)e^{-in\theta}}{\sqrt{2^n n!}\pi^{\frac{1}{4}}},
\end{equation}
where $H_n(x)$ is the (physical) Hermite polynomial of order $n$. In particular we will later use the fact that 
\begin{equation}\label{eq:overlapping}
    \braket{x_\theta}{0}_t=\pi^{-\frac{1}{4}}e^{-\frac{x_\theta^2}{2}},\text{ and that }\braket{x_\theta}{1}_t=\sqrt{2}x_\theta e^{-i\theta}\braket{x_\theta}{0}_t.
\end{equation}
Note that because the state of the environnement at time $t$ is the corresponding vacuum state, we have 
\begin{equation}\label{eq:initprob}
    p_{x_\theta}(t)=\abs{\braket{x_\theta}{0}_t}^2=\frac{e^{-x_\theta^2}}{\sqrt{\pi}}
\end{equation}

Continuous homodyne measurement is by definition the projection of the environment at each time on the quadrature states, giving the conditional (unnormalized) state
\begin{align}
    \bar{\rho}_c(t+\dt)&=\mathrm{Tr}\qty[U_I(t+\dt,t)\bm{\varrho}_c(t)U_I^\dagger(t+\dt,t)\qty(\mathbb{I}\otimes\ketbra{x_\theta})]\notag\\
    &= \expval{U_I(t+\dt,t)\qty[\rho_c(t)\otimes\ketbra{0}_t]U_I^\dagger(t+\dt,t)}{x_\theta},
\end{align}
with a corresponding normalization (probability)
\begin{equation}
    p_{x_\theta}(t+\dt) = \Tr{\bar{\rho}_c(t+\dt)}.
\end{equation}
We will now use the expansion of the Eq.~(\ref{eq:expansion}) to develop the previous expression. However, we truncate it to $o\qty(\sqrt{dt})$ because $\dt$ is negligible compared to $\sqrt{\dt}$, giving for the conditional unnormalized evolved state
\begin{equation}
    \bar{\rho}_c(t+\dt) = \abs{\braket{x_\theta}{0}_t}^2\rho_c(t) + \qty[c\rho_c(t)\braket{x_\theta}{1}_t\braket{0}{x_\theta}+\rho_c(t)c^\dagger\braket{x_\theta}{0}_t\braket{1}{x_\theta}]\sqrt{k\dt}+o\qty(\sqrt{\dt}).
\end{equation}
Simplifying this expression and factorizing it using Eq.~(\ref{eq:overlapping},\ref{eq:initprob}), we find
\begin{equation}\label{eq:homrhou}
    \bar{\rho}_c(t+\dt)= p_{x_\theta}(t)\qty{\rho_c(t)+x_\theta\sqrt{2k\dt}\qty[c\rho_c(t)e^{-i\theta}+\rho_c(t)c^\dagger e^{i\theta}]}+o\qty(\sqrt{dt}),
\end{equation}
with a normalization
\begin{align}
    p_{x_\theta}(t+\dt) &= p_{x_\theta}(t)\qty{1+x_\theta\sqrt{2k\dt}\expval{ce^{-i\theta}+c^\dagger e^{i\theta}}_t}+o\qty(\sqrt{\dt})\label{eq:homproblin}\\
    &\approx \frac{1}{\sqrt{\pi}}\times\exp\qty{-\qty[x_\theta-\sqrt{\frac{k\dt}{2}}\expval{ce^{-i\theta}+c^\dagger e^{i\theta}}_t]^2}.\label{eq:homprobexp}
\end{align}
\subsubsection{Wiener increments:}\noindent
We can deduce from Eq.~(\ref{eq:homprobexp}) that the probability to find the result $x_\theta$ at time $t+\dt$ corresponds to a Gaussian distribution\footnote{A Gaussian distribution is characterized by the following probability density function:\\$x\mapsto\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\sigma^2}}\exp\qty[-\frac{\qty(x-\mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}]$, where $\mu$ is the mean and $\sigma^2$ is the variance of the distribution.} (up to order $\sqrt{\dt}$). In this sense, the outcome of measurement is a random variable such that $x_\theta\hookrightarrow\mathcal{N}\qty(\sqrt{\frac{k\dt}{2}}\expval{ce^{-i\theta}+c^\dagger e^{i\theta}}_t, \frac{1}{2})$. By defining the random variable $\mathrm{d}y_t=\sqrt{2\dt}x_\theta$, we can calculate that $\mathbb{E}\qty[\mathrm{d}y_t]=\sqrt{k}\expval{ce^{-i\theta}+c^\dagger e^{i\theta}}_t\dt$ and $\mathbb{V}\qty[\mathrm{d}y_t]=\dt$. Consequently, we can statistically express this random variable as
\begin{equation}\label{eq:homodynesignal}
    \mathrm{d}y_t = \sqrt{k}\expval{ce^{-i\theta}+c^\dagger e^{i\theta}}_t\dt + \mathrm{d}w_t,    
\end{equation}
where $\mathrm{d}w_t$ is a classical Wiener increment, implying it follows the \ito~rule\footnote{This can be shown in the distribution meaning as $\int_0^t\mathrm{d}w_{t'}^2=\int_0^t\dt'$ which works for any interval $[0,t]$ so we can state that $\mathrm{d}w_t^2=\dt$~\cite{Jacobs2006}.}: $\mathrm{d}w_t^2=dt$. This property states that the random variable $\mathrm{d}w_t$, when being squared, becomes deterministic. Furthermore, the Wiener increment follows a Gaussian distribution, as observed in Eq.~(\ref{eq:homodynesignal}), characterized by $\mathbb{E}\qty[\mathrm{d}w_t]=0$ and $\mathbb{V}\qty[\mathrm{d}w_t]=\dt$. Experimentally, the quantity $y_T=\int_0^T\mathrm{d}y_t$ represents the integrated photo-current of homodyne detection; each $\mathrm{d}y_t$ is the infinitesimal recording of the measurement process at time $t$, which encodes (proportionally) the expectation value at time $t$ of the system quadrature $\expval{\frac{ce^{-i\theta}+c^\dagger e^{i\theta}}{\sqrt{2}}}_t$.
\subsubsection{Homodyne SME:}\noindent
The SME is found by inserting Eq.~(\ref{eq:homodynesignal}) into Eq.~(\ref{eq:homrhou},\ref{eq:homproblin}) which form the normalized evolved state. We can then expand the denominator and find
\begin{equation}{\label{eq:SMEhom1}}
    \mathrm{d}\rho_c(t) = \sqrt{k}\mathfrak{H}\qty[ce^{-i\theta}]\rho_c(t) \mathrm{d}w_t + o(\sqrt{\dt}).
\end{equation}
The terms of order $o(\dt)$ are obtained directly from the unconditional Lindblad master equation because averaging over all the measurement results gives back this equation. The average of Eq.~(\ref{eq:SMEhom1}) trivially gives 0, so the correct SME (in the Schrödinger picture) is
\begin{align}
    \boxed{\mathrm{d}\rho_c=-i\qty[H_0,\rho_c]\dt + k\mathfrak{D}[c]\rho_c\dt + \sqrt{k}\mathfrak{H}\qty[ce^{-i\theta}]\rho_c\mathrm{d}w_t},
\end{align}
where we detail that $\mathrm{d}w_t=\mathrm{d}y_t - \sqrt{k}\expval{ce^{-i\theta}+c^\dagger e^{i\theta}}_t \dt$. If the photo-detection SME unravelling was characterized by jump-like measurement, this one is called a \textit{diffusive} unravelling due to the impact of Wiener process in a diffusive term.
