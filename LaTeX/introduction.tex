\section{Theory of open quantum systems and continuous measurements}
\subsection{Introduction and motivations} \label{intro}

The paper you read will read is purposely non-exhaustive ; 
it consists of a pedagogical and demonstrative introduction to 
\textit{quantum continuous measurements}. Talented searchers made thorough and
detailed papers on the subjects we will tackle, but we wanted to allow mere students
or uninitiated people to understand the basics of weak measurements.

Quantum mechanics is the most fundamental physical theory humankind has developed 
due to accurate description at the smallest scale of the Universe. 
The primary results of quantum physics are obtained while considering a system isolated 
in the void from any perturbation or interaction. However, 
it seems trivial that any real physical system is ``feeling'' its surroundings 
(light rays or magnetic field hitting the system, coupling with a cavity, etc...) 
thus we ought to describe the impact of the environment on a system ; 
the couple System-Environment is called as \textit{Open} Quantum System (OQS).
Before diving more into this theory we should describe what we call a measurement \cite{Jacobs,WisemanThesis},
the postulate of quantum measurement states that we can choose any basis to represent
a system, living mathematically in an Hilbert space, 
$\ket{\psi}\in \mathcal{H}$: 
$$\ket{\psi} = \sum_n c_n \ket{\phi_n} \quad;\; c_n\in\mathbb{C}$$ 
We can look at which eigen-state $\ket{\phi_n}$ the system is in, 
doing so will (always) put the system in one of these states and we know the 
probability to get this state: $|c_n|^2$. This kind of measurement is called a 
\textit{von Neumann measurement} where the result is a state among the set of
eigen-states. Formally, it is a Projection-Valued Measurement (PVM) where the 
projector associated with the probability to find the result $n$ is 
$\mathbb{P}_n = \bra{\psi}P_n\ket{\psi}$. More general \textit{quantum measurement}
or Positive-Operator Valued Measures (POVM) $\Pi_n$ can be defined by
$\mathbb{P}_n = \Tr{\rho\Pi_n}$ where we use a generalization of quantum
states into density matrix $\rho$, which for pure states is 
$\rho_{pure}=\ket{\psi}\bra{\psi}$. This new formalism of density matrix has importance
revealed when dealing with OQS, please find Appendix \ref{Measurement_Theory} for further understanding.
